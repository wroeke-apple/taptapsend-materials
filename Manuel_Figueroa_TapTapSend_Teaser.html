If it is primarily a distribution problem, the path is fairly well-defined: map where the diaspora concentration is highest, identify the trust networks in those communities (churches, hometown associations, soccer leagues — the architecture varies by origin country), and build acquisition that travels through those networks rather than against them. People in these communities don't adopt financial products because of Instagram ads. They adopt them because someone they trust recommended them.

Part Two
What my thesis found — and why it's relevant here

My Lauder master's thesis examined why remittances in El Salvador — roughly 20% of GDP, sent predominantly from the US — fail to generate the financial inclusion that development economists predict they should. I combined World Bank Enterprise Survey data from 2016 and 2023 with four case studies and field research interviews in-country. The findings are directly relevant to TapTapSend's neobank thesis.

"From Violence to Vulnerability: MiPYMEs and Structural Challenges in El Salvador's New Economic Landscape"

66 pages. Four case studies. Field interviews in-country. The core question: why do large remittance inflows not translate into financial inclusion or SME growth at the recipient end?
The cash-out problem

60% of El Salvador remittances flow through banks, but only ~3% of recipients keep funds in accounts. Most withdraw immediately — the formal financial system is a pass-through, not a relationship.

Trust is the blocking variable

Recipients prefer cash not for convenience but because of deep institutional distrust — bank fees, past negative experiences, cultural norms. Product design has to address trust before features.

The dependency trap

Regular remittance inflows can paradoxically suppress formal credit demand. Households with steady transfers are less likely to seek business loans — the "remittances as financial on-ramp" thesis hasn't materialized.

Security ≠ financial inclusion

Post-Bukele security improvements dramatically improved business sentiment — crime dropped from 28% to 5% as top business obstacle. But access to finance replaced it immediately as constraint #1.

What this means for TapTapSend

The neobank pivot requires that remittance recipients become ongoing financial services customers. My research describes in detail why that transition is structurally hard — and what conditions would need to be true for it to work. That's useful context for building the product bridge, not a reason to be pessimistic about it.
