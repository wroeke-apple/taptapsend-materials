00 · Overview

The Sender Segments That Fall Outside the Standard Funnel

TapTapSend's current acquisition model assumes a sender who has a US bank account or a standard debit/credit card and is comfortable completing an identity verification flow online. That's a meaningful share of the diaspora remittance market, but not all of it. Three sender cohorts fall systematically outside this funnel, not because they don't send money to Central America, but because the product doesn't accommodate how they access and hold money.

The analysis focuses on CA3 — El Salvador, Guatemala, and Honduras — TapTapSend's three Central American corridors. The sizing is built from public data — Pew Research, FDIC, IRS, World Bank, SSA — with conservative assumptions layered in. The goal is not to claim precision but to establish whether each segment is large enough to warrant a strategic response. In all three cases, it is.

01 · Segment One
01

The Prepaid / Unbanked Sender

Diaspora workers who receive pay in cash or on prepaid debit cards and cannot fund a TapTapSend transfer today

TapTapSend does not accept prepaid debit cards as a funding method. This is a standard risk/compliance decision (prepaid cards carry higher fraud rates), but it has a real cost. A meaningful portion of the Salvadoran, Guatemalan, and Honduran diaspora in the US is unbanked or underbanked, particularly in the construction, agriculture, domestic work, and food service sectors where cash and payroll prepaid cards dominate.

FDIC's 2023 National Survey found 9.5% of Hispanic households in the US are unbanked, compared to 1.9% of white households. For the Central American diaspora specifically — El Salvador, Guatemala, Honduras — the rate is significantly higher: older FDIC research found roughly 64% of Salvadoran remittance senders are unbanked, consistent with the broader finding that only 2 in 10 Central American migrants have bank accounts (IDB). Western Union and MoneyGram capture this cohort today through cash-in agent networks. TapTapSend has no equivalent.

The Product Gap

TapTapSend explicitly does not accept prepaid cards. A Salvadoran construction worker paid on a Green Dot or Netspend card cannot send money home through TapTapSend today. Western Union can serve him at the grocery store counter. This is not a small edge case — it is likely the majority of the lowest-income, highest-frequency sender segment in the CA3 corridor.

What It Would Take

Risk mitigation

Prepaid card fraud is higher — the standard fix is velocity limits (e.g., max $300/day for prepaid-funded sends), additional identity verification steps for first send, and a 24-hour hold on first transaction. Remitly and WorldRemit both accept major prepaid networks (Visa/MC prepaid) with these guardrails.

Cash-in network (longer term)

True cash-in — accepting a cash deposit at a partner location to fund a send — would unlock the fully unbanked segment. This is harder (requires agent network or partnership with Green Dot, PayNearMe, or similar). Worth a feasibility assessment but not a 90-day priority.

02 · Segment Two
02

The ITIN Holder — Undocumented & Non-SSN Sender

~14M undocumented immigrants in the US, disproportionately Central American, who may not clear TapTapSend's standard SSN-based identity verification

Pew Research estimates 14 million unauthorized immigrants lived in the US as of 2023 — a record high, up from 10.5M in 2021. The IRS issues Individual Taxpayer Identification Numbers (ITINs) to individuals who have federal tax obligations but cannot obtain a Social Security Number. As of 2021, approximately 5.4 million active ITINs were in circulation, with the vast majority held by undocumented immigrants and their dependents.

This population sends remittances at higher rates and as a higher share of income than documented immigrants — the research consensus is that undocumented immigrants remit more, not less, because they are more likely to have immediate family abroad they are supporting and less likely to have brought family to the US. The CA3 corridor is disproportionately represented: Salvadoran, Guatemalan, and Honduran communities have high concentrations of undocumented workers relative to other nationalities.

The Maza Precedent

Maza — backed by a16z, acquired by Flex for $40M in April 2025 — built a US bank account and Visa debit card product specifically for ITIN holders, requiring no SSN. It grew to 250,000 customers at 290% YoY revenue. The product worked because it solved the identity verification problem for a population that was excluded from almost every mainstream financial product. TapTapSend doesn't need to build the bank account — it needs to accept the ITIN as a valid identity credential for account creation and KYC, the same way Maza did on the deposit side.

Compliance Considerations

Is ITIN-based KYC permissible?

Yes. FinCEN's Customer Identification Program (CIP) rules allow ITINs as an acceptable form of tax identification for US persons without SSNs. Several licensed MSBs and neobanks (Maza, Comun, a16z-backed Majority) have built ITIN-compliant KYC flows. TapTapSend's existing FinCEN MSB license does not prohibit this — it requires a documented CIP program, which can include ITIN as a valid ID.

Risk profile

ITIN holders send to known family members in known corridors with consistent ticket sizes and frequency. In practice, this cohort has lower fraud rates than anonymous prepaid card users because the identity link is stronger. The risk is more reputational (serving undocumented population) than financial — a board-level conversation, not a compliance blocker.

03 · Segment Three
03

The US Expat Retiree in Latin America

760K+ Americans receive Social Security while living abroad; a growing cohort has retired to El Salvador, Guatemala, Panama, Mexico, and Costa Rica, and struggles to move money efficiently

This is a structurally different segment from the diaspora remittance sender, and it requires a different product motion. US retirees living in LatAm are not sending money home to family. They are moving their own money — Social Security payments deposited to a US bank account, investment income, pension distributions — into a local currency account to cover living expenses, real estate, healthcare, and daily costs.

The pain is real and well-documented. US banks frequently freeze or close accounts when they detect a foreign address. Wire fees run $30–50 per transfer. FX spreads at US banks add another 2–4%. Many retirees end up using ATM withdrawals (expensive) or informal arrangements (risky). The SSA pays approximately $6.1B annually in benefits to roughly 760,000 beneficiaries living outside the US (SSA, 2022). Mexico alone hosts an estimated 1.6 million Americans (State Dept) — the largest expat population in the world — while El Salvador, Panama, Costa Rica, and Guatemala are smaller but growing fast, driven by low cost of living and an increasingly active online discovery channel.

Why This Is Interesting for TapTapSend

TapTapSend already has the licensed corridors, the payout rails, and the regulatory footprint. The product is built for person-to-person transfers to LatAm. The retiree use case is the same rails, different narrative: "send yourself money to live on" rather than "send family money to support them." The average ticket is larger ($800–1,500/month vs. $180–200 for diaspora senders), frequency is lower (monthly vs. 8–10x/year), but LTV is potentially higher and CAC is very different — retiree expat communities are tight-knit, active on Facebook groups and expat forums, and respond to peer recommendations.

GTM for This Segment Is Different

Channel: expat forums & Facebook groups

US retirees in El Salvador, Guatemala, and Mexico are intensely community-organized online. Facebook groups like "Expats in El Salvador" and "Americans in Guatemala" have tens of thousands of active members and extremely high trust in peer recommendations. One authentic post from a satisfied user in these groups is worth more than any paid ad. CAC in this channel could be under $20.

Product consideration: "send yourself" framing

The current TapTapSend product is framed entirely around sending to family. The retiree use case needs a subtle repositioning — "move your money to live on" — without a full product rebuild. A landing page, some adjusted copy, and a slightly different onboarding flow (no "recipient's name" assumption baked in) would be the minimum viable change.

04 · Combined View

What These Three Segments Are Worth Together

Each segment requires a different product or compliance response — prepaid card acceptance, ITIN-based KYC, and expat-oriented positioning — but none of them requires building a new product from scratch. TapTapSend already has the corridors, the licenses, and the payout rails. These are activation gaps, not product gaps.

The Bigger Point

The combined incremental revenue across these three segments — ~$5.0M annually in base case — is roughly 2.5x what the CA3 corridor activation plan projects in Year 1 from the standard diaspora sender GTM. Note: the expat retiree segment is the smallest near-term revenue opportunity of the three, and the population estimate for CA3 specifically carries the most uncertainty — the real strategic case for this segment is proof of concept for a product motion (self-transfer) that scales dramatically if extended to Mexico's 1.6M American residents. These segments don't replace the core activation strategy; they expand the addressable population of it. And because the CAC for all three is lower than paid digital acquisition (community-driven, forum-driven, word-of-mouth), they improve blended unit economics across the portfolio.
